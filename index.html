01
Australia's Migration Wave

How migration surged, collapsed,
and rebounded

Australia's net overseas migration has been one of the most volatile among developed nations. A resource-boom peak in 2008–09, a decade of steady growth, a sudden COVID collapse — and then an unprecedented rebound that reshaped cities and strained housing markets almost overnight.

Migration to Australia did not grow in a straight line. The two decades from 2004 to 2024 can be read as three distinct chapters. The first, from 2004 to 2009, was a mining-boom era that pulled workers from every corner of the globe: net migration climbed from 161,280 in 2004–05 to a then-record 302,220 in 2008–09, fuelled by construction, resources, and international education. The Global Financial Crisis ended that era abruptly — by 2010–11 net migration had retreated to 188,570.

The second chapter was a long plateau. Through the 2010s, migration settled into a rhythm of roughly 200,000–280,000 per year. Policy was tightening — the permanent visa program was capped, student visa conditions were toughened — but demand held firm. India, China, and South-East Asia were sending increasing numbers of students who would become skilled visa holders and, eventually, permanent residents.

The third chapter began on 20 March 2020, when Australia closed its international borders. The result was historic: net migration fell to −92,460 in 2020–21 — the first negative reading in modern records. Campuses emptied, hospitality kitchens lost their workforce, and aged care facilities struggled for staff. The human cost of closed borders was felt across every industry that had come to depend on migrant labour.

What followed was equally historic. When borders reopened in late 2021, years of pent-up demand — students who had deferred enrolments, skilled workers whose visas had lapsed, families reunited — arrived in a single concentrated wave. By 2022–23, net migration had surged to 564,560, more than triple the pre-pandemic level and the highest figure Australia had ever recorded. The wave is now moderating: 2024–25 stands at 322,550, still well above the long-run average of around 200,000 per year.

A two-city story

The map confirms what residents of Sydney and Melbourne already feel: migration is highly concentrated geographically. In 2024–25, New South Wales received 91,550 net migrants (30% of the national total) and Victoria 87,750 (29%). Together these two states absorbed nearly three in five of all arrivals, despite containing just under half of Australia's total population.

Queensland is the emerging third destination, capturing 18% — up from 15% in 2018–19 — as housing affordability pressures in Sydney and Melbourne push both domestic migrants and new arrivals northward. Western Australia (13%), boosted by continued resources sector demand, rounds out the top four. Tasmania (1%) and the Northern Territory (1%) remain marginal recipients despite active state government recruitment programs.

Reading the line chart

The line chart highlights how Australia’s migration pattern changed across major global events. During the Global Financial Crisis, migration still remained relatively stable, peaking at around 302k in 2008–09 before easing slightly in the following years. In contrast, the COVID-19 border closure caused an unprecedented collapse, pushing net migration below zero in 2020–21 for the first time in modern records.

The sharp rebound in 2022–23 reflects the reopening of borders and the release of delayed migration demand from students, workers, and skilled migrants. Rather than a slow recovery, migration surged to a record 565k in a single year, showing how quickly international movement resumed once restrictions were removed. Although the latest figures indicate moderation after the peak, migration levels remain historically high compared with most of the past two decades.

Permanent, temporary and citizen movement over time

Each coloured band represents a different traveller category. Temporary visas (mid-blue) form the largest and most volatile layer — their collapse in 2020–21 and explosion in 2022–23 drove the overall migration story.

The stacked area chart reveals a structural shift that headline numbers obscure. In 2004–05, permanent visas accounted for 25% of total arrivals and temporary visas for 43%. By 2018–19, permanent visas had shrunk to just 16% while temporary visas had grown to 64%. Australia's migration system had quietly transformed into a temporary-first model.

The COVID year is striking for a different reason: temporary visas did not just fall — they collapsed to just 20% of total arrivals (29,580 people), while permanent visas temporarily rose to 26% simply because they were processed domestically. The pandemic revealed how dependent the system had become on physical mobility, particularly of students and working holiday makers.

In 2022–23, temporary visa holders made up 75% of all arrivals — 556,620 people — against only 80,390 permanent visa grants. This ratio represents a fundamental question about Australia's migration architecture: a system designed to be temporary is increasingly functioning as the primary pathway to permanent residence, with all the uncertainty and exploitation risk that entails for migrants who hold temporary status for years.

02
Where Did Migrants Come From?

From South Asia to Oceania —
tracing the origins

Australia's migrant origins have undergone a quiet revolution over two decades. The United Kingdom and Europe, once the dominant source regions, have been overtaken by South and South-East Asia. Today, a majority of arrivals come from countries that barely registered in the statistics twenty years ago.

In 2004–05, North-West Europe (led by the UK) was the single largest source region. By 2014–15, South-East Asia had overtaken it. By 2018–19, Southern & Central Asia — driven almost entirely by India — had surged to 140,200 net arrivals, more than twice North-West Europe's 50,260. The shift is structural, not cyclical: it reflects India's growing middle class, the expansion of Australian university campuses, and active recruitment of Indian nurses, engineers, and IT workers under the skilled migration program.

The COVID period temporarily scrambled the rankings but did not reverse them. When borders reopened, Southern & Central Asia came back first and hardest. By 2022–23, the region contributed 95,030 net migrants from India alone — a single-country figure that exceeded the entire contribution of North-East Asia (China, Japan, Korea combined). By 2024–25, India (57,410) remained #1, followed by China (35,160), the UK (25,620), and New Zealand (24,320).

The country comparison

The grouped bar chart highlights shifts that aggregate regional data obscures. India's absolute dominance is clear — 73,190 in 2018–19, falling slightly to 57,410 in 2024–25 as the government tightened student visa conditions — but the composition beneath it has changed significantly. China (35,160) has recovered strongly from its COVID nadir, while the Philippines (19,440) has moved up in the rankings as aged care and nursing recruitment intensified. The UK and New Zealand — traditional sources — have both grown in absolute terms, a consequence of post-Brexit British mobility and Trans-Tasman movement responding to New Zealand's housing market pressures.

Reading the lollipop chart

The lollipop chart reveals that the fastest-growing regions are not the largest ones. Oceania's 72.7% growth (29,980 → 51,780) reflects expanded Pacific labour mobility agreements. Sub-Saharan Africa's 64.5% growth (19,140 → 31,490) comes from a small base but represents an emerging corridor — South African, Nigerian, and Zimbabwean professionals increasingly feature in skilled and humanitarian programs. Southern & Eastern Europe grew just 1.4%, the slowest of any region, as EU mobility rights reduce the incentive for Europeans to pursue Australian permanent residency.

Who Were the Migrants? People & Work

Young, working-age — and
reshaping Australia's workforce

Migration is not just a headline number. Behind the statistics are real people: students filling university lecture theatres, nurses working overnight shifts, chefs running commercial kitchens, and engineers on remote mining sites. The data reveals a workforce story as much as a population one — and one marked by stark economic inequality depending on which visa you hold.

The age profile of migrants is heavily concentrated in the 18–29 band, which consistently accounts for the largest share of arrivals. This is not accidental — it is the structural consequence of Australia's student visa system. International education is one of Australia's largest service exports, worth over $40 billion annually before COVID, and the student visa pipeline feeds directly into graduate visas, then skilled temporary visas, and eventually permanent residency for a significant subset of graduates.

But the age concentration tells only part of the story. What migrants do when they arrive — and what they earn — varies dramatically by visa type. A Permanent Skilled migrant working in Mining earned a median of $130,315 in 2022–23. A Temporary Skilled migrant in the same industry earned even more: $139,316. Meanwhile, a Working Holiday Maker in Hospitality — the largest employment category for that visa — earned just $4,400 for the year. These figures represent not different skill levels or work ethics, but different visa architectures: one designed to attract the highly paid, the other to provide seasonal labour at the margins of the wage system.

Across all visa types combined, migrant workers held approximately 6.2 million jobs in 2022–23 — a figure that encompasses everyone from the 458,208 Health & Social Care workers on Permanent Skilled visas to the 216,268 Temporary Students working in Hospitality. Understanding who those workers are and what they earn matters not just for migration policy but for minimum wage enforcement, housing allocation, and the design of social services.

The bubble chart: two economies in one country

The bubble chart is designed to be used interactively. When set to Permanent Skilled, the bubbles are distributed across the full income range — Mining sits at $130,315, Finance at $75,662, Health at $44,108 — and the largest bubbles (Health at 458,208 jobs, Professional Services at 292,873 jobs) sit in the mid-income range. The picture is of a diversified workforce distributed across the economy.

Switch to Working Holiday Maker and the picture transforms completely. Every bubble collapses to the left side of the chart — all incomes below $25,000. The largest bubble is Hospitality at 62,892 jobs and just $4,400 median income. The second largest is Agriculture at 25,246 jobs and $5,171. These are the industries that fruit-picking seasons and ski resorts run on — legal under the Working Holiday framework, but representing a structural dependence on low-cost migrant labour that has resisted policy reform for decades.

The normalised area: Health's quiet dominance

The normalised area chart tells a slower story. Admin & Support Services was the largest migrant employment sector in 2018–19 at 16%, but its share has edged downward. Health & Social Care has grown steadily from 14% to 17% — now the largest single employer of migrants. This reflects two converging forces: Australia's ageing population creating demand for aged care workers, and active government recruitment of nurses and carers from the Philippines, India, and South Africa under targeted visa streams. Hospitality (13%) and Professional Services (10%) remain stable, pointing to entrenched patterns of migrant employment in services industries. Smaller industries such as Construction and Manufacturing changed only slightly over time, reinforcing the idea that migrant employment patterns are generally stable even during periods of economic disruption and recovery.

Why Did They Come?

Skilled workers, students, families —
the visa story

Visa categories are the hidden architecture of migration. They determine not just who can come to Australia, but what they can do when they arrive, how long they can stay, and whether they can eventually call it home. Understanding the visa system is understanding why migration looks the way it does — and why it sometimes fails the people it is supposed to serve.

Australia operates two parallel migration systems. The permanent program is capped and closely managed: a fixed number of visas allocated each year across skilled, family, and humanitarian categories. It peaked at 186,258 grants in 2015–16 and has been deliberately held around 185,000 since 2022–23, despite demand far exceeding supply. Skilled visas consistently account for roughly 70% of permanent grants — 132,148 out of 185,001 in 2024–25 — reflecting a clear policy priority: economic contribution above family reunion.

The temporary stream operates without a cap. Student visas, Working Holiday visas, skilled temporary visas, and visitor visas are demand-driven — anyone who meets the criteria can apply, and the government manages numbers through eligibility conditions rather than quotas. This asymmetry has produced the situation visible in the heatmap: in 2022–23, student visa net flows alone (262,270) exceeded the entire permanent program (195,004 grants). The student visa had effectively become Australia's largest migration program, with the government later intervening to tighten conditions as housing costs and infrastructure capacity became politically sensitive.

One year in the data stands apart: 2020–21. The heatmap row for that year is almost entirely orange across every visa type — every category recording outflows as international travel collapsed. Student visa net flow dropped to −60,680 (students left and were not replaced). Working Holiday makers went to −6,800. Even Skilled Temporary recorded −2,370. The pattern reversed just as dramatically one year later, confirming that the COVID disruption was a temporary shock rather than a structural change in demand.

The permanent program: a managed system under pressure

The stacked bar chart reveals the remarkable stability of Australia's permanent visa program. The skilled:family ratio has held at approximately 70:30 for a decade — a political equilibrium between employer lobby groups demanding more skilled workers and community advocates pushing for family reunion. The only significant deviation was 2020–21, when skilled grants fell to 79,620 (51%) and family grants rose to 77,372 (49%) — not because family demand surged, but because skilled migration requires physical arrival while family visas can be processed onshore. The ratio snapped back to 73% skilled in 2022–23 as skilled workers arrived in volume.

The heatmap: reading policy through colour

The heatmap is a policy timeline. The student visa dominates the blue end — consistently the largest net inflow category. The Working Holiday row shows strong positive flows until 2019–20, then the COVID orange of 2020–21, then a rapid recovery. The Visitors row is unusual: its largest positive flow (100,860 in 2019–20) happened just before borders closed, as tourists and business visitors who entered in early 2020 could not leave. The 2022–23 column — all deep blue — tells the reopening story in a single glance.

💡 The 2020–21 row in the heatmap is the only year where every single visa category shows orange (net outflow). Student: −60,680. Working Holiday: −6,800. Skilled Temporary: −2,370. Visitors: −7,320. It remains the most complete picture of what closed borders actually meant for migration flows.

The heatmap closes the story with a question as much as a conclusion. Australia's migration system is not a smooth, managed flow — it is a series of policy-driven lurches responding to economic cycles, public sentiment, housing pressure, and global events. The student visa net flow hit 262,270 in 2022–23, then fell to 108,070 in 2024–25 as the government tightened conditions. The permanent program cap barely moved across a decade despite record demand. Working holiday inflows ebbed and flowed with the seasons and with bilateral visa agreements that few Australians know exist. Behind every number in every chart is a decision — made in Canberra or in a consulate overseas — that shaped whether someone got to come, stay, or go.
